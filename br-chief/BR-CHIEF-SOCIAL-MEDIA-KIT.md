## BIOGRAPHY (LONG — FOR PRESS, SONGS, WEBSITE)

Derek "D-Chief" Theriot was born September 3, 2000, on the Northside of Baton Rouge, Louisiana — specifically the Florida Boulevard corridor. While most of Louisiana's rap attention flows to New Orleans and the Houston border, Derek watched Baton Rouge get overlooked city after city. Talented BR rappers would try to sound like somebody else, fail, and fade. He decided to try something different.

He was going to make the world come to Baton Rouge.

At 16, he recorded his first track — "Handle It" — in his mother's house on Florida Blvd. 47 plays on SoundCloud. All friends and family. He didn't stop. Track by track, show by show, BR started paying attention. By the time he was 22, BR Chief was the guy local clubs called to fill the room. By 24, people from Houston and ATL were reaching out. By 25, he had three tracks ready to go — "This Dancing After," "Florida Blvd," and "Handle It" — and a vision: make BR the most recognizable city in Louisiana, then take it national.

The BR Chief sound is not imitating anyone. It's chopped-and-screwed roots (Paul Wall, Mike Jones), Wayne swagger (smooth, effortless, generational), Drake melody (hooks that stick), grungy trap drums (Trapwire-era aggression), and Louisiana brass undertone. The result is something that sounds like Baton Rouge specifically — LSU purple and gold, late night techno clubs, Florida Blvd cruising, Tiger Stadium energy, and the grit of a city that doesn't flinch.

His tagline: **"Handle it. Then dance after."** — meaning: put in the work first, then celebrate. The grind comes before the glory. BR Chief doesn't do one without the other.

His first three tracks represent three sides of BR Chief:
- **"Florida Blvd"** — Street love, nostalgia, the city that raised him
- **"Handle It"** — The grind, the mission, the motto
- **"This Dancing After"** — The reward, the swagger, the celebration
